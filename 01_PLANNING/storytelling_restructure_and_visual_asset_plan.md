# ThermoGuard AI Storytelling Restructure and Visual Asset Plan

## 1. Current-Fit Summary

The current ThermoGuard AI homepage already has a strong B2B foundation. It presents the product as a continuous thermal-risk monitoring platform, uses credible operational language, includes dashboard-style proof, and speaks to facility, maintenance, data centre, property, industrial, and service-provider audiences.

What works well:

- The visual direction is already premium enough for a technical B2B product: dark interface, dashboard mockups, risk states, telemetry cues, and restrained motion.
- The site has useful commercial sections: use cases, integrations, plans, comparison, and a contact/demo CTA.
- The dashboard and risk components make the product feel more concrete than a generic SaaS landing page.

Where the current structure is weaker:

- The homepage explains the product in many separate pieces instead of one clear product journey.
- Several sections repeat similar ideas: live monitoring, anomaly detection, risk scoring, alerts, recommended actions, and maintenance evidence.
- Dashboard proof appears in multiple places, which is useful but risks making the page feel heavy before the customer understands the overall system.
- The current plans section is more deployment-pathway oriented than pricing-oriented, so the commercial offer still lacks a clear visible starting point.

Why the storytelling direction improves the experience:

- A dedicated Product Experience section can become the central explanatory engine of the site.
- It can show the full journey from physical panel risk to sensors, telemetry, AI interpretation, risk state, alert, action, and evidence.
- Other sections can then become shorter and more focused because they no longer need to re-explain the entire product.
- The result should feel more premium and easier to understand without losing B2B credibility.

## 4. Product Experience Section Strategy

Proposed section name:

- Primary recommendation: "How ThermoGuard AI Works"
- Alternate title: "From Heat Signal to Maintenance Action"

Purpose:

- Make the product journey instantly understandable.
- Connect physical electrical-panel risk to software intelligence and maintenance action.
- Become the main explanatory section so other sections can be shorter.

Placement:

- After the hero/value/problem setup.
- Before RiskIntelligence, DashboardPreview, UseCases, Integrations, and Pricing.

Recommended layout concept:

- Hybrid sticky storytelling section.
- Left or centre: a sticky visual scene that changes across steps.
- Right or below: concise step copy tied to each visual state.
- On desktop, use a sticky visual frame with scroll-linked steps.
- On mobile, use stacked step cards with the visual repeated or simplified.

Storytelling flow:

1. Hidden thermal risk exists inside a panel, switchgear cabinet, or critical asset.
2. Heat points are highlighted to show where risk forms.
3. Sensors appear at the monitored points.
4. Telemetry lines move from the field asset into the ThermoGuard AI platform.
5. The AI layer interprets baseline behaviour, drift, persistence, and anomaly strength.
6. The asset receives a clear state: Normal, Watch, Elevated, or Critical.
7. An alert is routed to the right owner.
8. A maintenance action is created or recommended.
9. Evidence and history are stored for reporting and inspection.

User experience while scrolling:

- The visitor should feel like one visual story is unfolding, not like they are reading a list of features.
- Each scroll step should reveal one new layer of the system.
- Text should remain short and specific.
- The final frame should show the complete system: physical asset, live telemetry, AI/risk state, alert/action, and evidence record.

Recommended section structure:

- Opening line: one sentence explaining the end-to-end journey.
- Sticky visual frame: panel illustration + telemetry + platform UI.
- Step list: 7-9 short steps, each no more than 1-2 sentences.
- Closing proof strip: "Continuous monitoring", "Risk state classification", "Routed alerts", "Evidence history".

## 5. Visual Assets Strategy

Recommended approach:

- Use a hybrid of CSS/SVG layered illustration and existing React dashboard mock components.
- Avoid heavy video, large image sequences, or canvas frame playback for the first implementation.
- Use vector/layered UI assets that can be controlled by scroll state and CSS transitions.

Why this fits the current codebase:

- The project already uses React components, Tailwind classes, lucide icons, CSS variables, and mock UI components.
- Existing assets like RiskBadge, ThermalMap, TrendChart, DashboardMock, and dashboard card patterns can be reused.
- A lightweight layered scene is easier to maintain, responsive, and accessible than a rendered video sequence.

Likely visual sequence:

1. Environment / panel frame
   - A stylised electrical panel or switchgear cabinet.
   - Dark technical frame consistent with the current UI.

2. Heat points
   - Glow points appear on terminals or priority zones.
   - Use risk colours sparingly: normal green, watch blue, elevated amber, critical red.

3. Sensor layer
   - Small sensor markers attach to highlighted heat points.
   - Labels stay minimal.

4. Telemetry flow
   - Lines or pulses move from panel to platform.
   - Use CSS transitions and opacity/transform changes.

5. AI platform layer
   - A compact AI/risk engine card appears.
   - Show baseline, drift, anomaly score, or risk score in simplified visual form.

6. Risk classification
   - Normal / Watch / Elevated / Critical badges appear.
   - Reuse existing RiskBadge component.

7. Alert and action
   - Alert card appears with owner/status.
   - Maintenance action card appears with a recommended inspection/action.

8. Evidence history
   - Timeline or log card appears.
   - Show closed-loop proof: detected, assigned, inspected, recorded.

Assets to create:

- `src/components/site/ProductExperienceSection.tsx`
- `src/components/site/product-experience/PanelScene.tsx`
- `src/components/site/product-experience/TelemetryLayer.tsx`

Asset organisation:

- Keep component assets in `src/components/site/product-experience/`.
- If static SVGs are used, place them in `src/assets/product-experience/`.
- Prefer component-native SVG/CSS for elements that need animation.
- Avoid generating bitmap assets unless a polished panel/environment image is specifically approved.

Current component reuse:

- `RiskBadge` for state labels.
- `ThermalMap` patterns for heat-point logic or visual language.
- `TrendChart` or `Sparkline` for platform telemetry cues.
- Existing `panel`, `panel-strong`, `panel-inset`, `grid-bg`, and risk colour CSS utilities.

## 6. Animation and Interaction Approach

Recommended strategy:

- Use a mixed approach:
  - CSS transitions for reveal, opacity, transform, pulses, lines, and card state.
  - A small custom scroll-step observer for desktop sticky storytelling.
  - Avoid GSAP / ScrollTrigger in the first pass unless the design later needs timeline-level control.
  - Avoid Framer Motion unless the project intentionally adds it as a dependency.
  - Avoid canvas frame playback for now.

Why:

- The current project already has a `useReveal` hook and CSS reveal utilities.
- Adding a heavy animation dependency for one section increases maintenance cost.
- The desired effect can be achieved with sticky positioning, IntersectionObserver, CSS variables, and state-driven class changes.

Desktop behaviour:

- Sticky visual frame remains in view while the user scrolls through step text.
- Each active step toggles visual layers.
- Transitions should be smooth but subtle: fades, line drawing, small transforms, and badge changes.

Mobile behaviour:

- No sticky scene.
- Use stacked cards with a simplified visual at the top or per step.
- Keep animation limited to reveal and small state highlights.

Reduced-motion fallback:

- Disable pulse animations and scroll-linked transitions.
- Show the complete final visual or static step visuals.
- Preserve all content without requiring animation to understand the product.

Performance considerations:

- Keep the scene DOM small.
- Use transform and opacity for motion.
- Avoid large images, videos, and layout-thrashing scroll handlers.
- Use IntersectionObserver rather than continuous scroll calculations where possible.

Guardrail:

- Animation should explain sequence and causality. It should not become a visual showpiece detached from the product message.

## 7. Pricing / Plans Section Strategy

Recommendation:

- Refine the existing `PlansSection` into the main Pricing / Plans section instead of creating a separate parallel commercial section.

Why:

- The current Starter / Professional / Enterprise structure is already a useful commercial foundation.
- Replacing it completely risks unnecessary churn.
- Adding a separate PricingSection next to PlansSection would create duplication and confusion.

Recommended pricing structure:

1. Starter / Core Monitoring
   - Show visible base price placeholder if business price is not approved.
   - Example: "Starting from £X/month" with "Placeholder pending business confirmation" clearly marked.
   - Include asset scope, basic dashboard, risk states, alerts, evidence log, and onboarding support.

2. Professional
   - For multi-panel or multi-site operations.
   - Keep as quote-based or "From £X/month" only if approved.
   - Include portfolio visibility, alert lifecycle, reporting exports, and expanded support.

3. Enterprise / Custom Deployment
   - For custom integrations, BMS / SCADA / CMMS workflows, advanced reporting, escalation models, and managed rollout support.
   - CTA: "Request custom quote" or "Discuss deployment".

Duplication avoidance:

- Do not add a second pricing block elsewhere.
- Rename `PlansSection` to either `PricingPlansSection` or keep the component name but change the section title and anchor to `pricing`.
- Keep deployment scoping language as supporting copy, not as a separate full commercial section.
- Final CTA should reference demo and quote but not repeat all plan details.

## 11. Risks and Guardrails

Do not let the page become too long.

- The Product Experience section should replace repeated explanation, not add another layer of repetition.

Do not create duplicate commercial sections.

- Pricing and Plans should become one section.
- Avoid separate Starter / Professional / Enterprise and Core / Custom blocks unless they are merged into one coherent pricing model.

Do not let pricing be vague or conflicting.

- Show a visible base price only if approved or clearly marked as a placeholder.
- Make custom pricing route obvious.
- Do not state invented pricing as fact.

Do not let motion become distracting.

- Use motion to explain sequence.
- Keep transitions restrained.
- Ensure the story works without animation.

Do not break mobile usability.

- Use a simplified stacked version on mobile.
- Avoid horizontal storytelling that requires precise scroll behaviour on small screens.

Do not weaken B2B credibility.

- Avoid playful, consumer-style gimmicks.
- Keep language operational, specific, and commercially useful.

Do not turn the redesign into a style exercise.

- Every visual frame must answer a customer question:
  - What is being monitored?
  - What data is collected?
  - What does AI do?
  - What risk state is produced?
  - Who is alerted?
  - What action follows?
  - What evidence is stored?

Do not copy Apple.

- Use Apple-like techniques only as inspiration: focused storytelling, controlled visual reveals, and strong hierarchy.
- The design should remain ThermoGuard AI's own technical B2B product experience.
